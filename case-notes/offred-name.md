This case focuses on the name "Offred" as a textual cue. The case shows how the Model Reader reconstructs implicit information about hidden personal identity, institutional naming, possession, and interpretive withholding.

The case is based on three textual moments:

1. the secret exchange of personal names at the Red Centre;
2. Offred's statement that "Offred" is not her real name;
3. the Historical Notes' explanation of the patronymic structure of names such as "Offred", "Ofglen", and "Ofwarren".

## Overview

In *The Handmaid's Tale*, the name "Offred" is not simply a character name. It functions as an interpretive cue. The reader gradually understands that it is an institutional name imposed by Gilead, while the narrator's original personal name remains hidden and unavailable.

This case is useful for our ontology because it shows how interpretation depends on implicit information. The text does not fully state the narrator's original identity, but it gives the Model Reader enough cues to infer that another name exists and that its absence is meaningful.

## Passage 1: Secret exchange of names

**Source:** Margaret Atwood, *The Handmaid's Tale*, PDF p. 14.

**Micro-quote:**  
> "we exchanged names"

**Working passage:**  
> "We learned to whisper almost without sound. In the semi-darkness we could stretch out our arms, when the Aunts weren’t looking, and touch each other’s hands across space. We learned to lip-read, our heads flat on the beds, turned sideways, watching each other’s mouths. In this way we exchanged names, from bed to bed: Alma. Janine. Dolores. Moira. June."

**Contextual paraphrase:**  
At the Red Centre, the Handmaids secretly exchange their personal names in whispers, from bed to bed. This moment presents names as forbidden but still preserved traces of personal identity. The act of exchanging names becomes a small form of resistance against Gilead's attempt to erase individual identity.

**Interpretive function:**  
This passage prepares the reader to understand that personal names still exist beneath the institutional names imposed by Gilead. It also introduces the possibility that one of the names in the list may be connected to the narrator, without explicitly confirming it.

**Function in the ontology:**  
This passage can be modeled as a textual fragment that activates knowledge about personal names, secrecy, and identity preservation.

## Passage 2: Offred's hidden real name

**Source:** Margaret Atwood, *The Handmaid's Tale*, PDF p. 87.

**Micro-quote:**  
> "My name isn't Offred"

**Working passage:**  
> "My name isn’t Offred, I have another name, which nobody uses now because it’s forbidden. I tell myself it doesn’t matter, your name is like your telephone number, useful only to others; but what I tell myself is wrong, it does matter. I keep the knowledge of this name like something hidden, some treasure I’ll come back to dig up, one day. I think of this name as buried. This name has an aura around it, like an amulet, some charm that’s survived from an unimaginably distant past. I lie in my single bed at night, with my eyes closed, and the name floats there behind my eyes, not quite within reach, shining in the dark.

**Contextual paraphrase:**  
Offred explicitly states that "Offred" is not her real name. She says that she has another name, but that this name is forbidden and no longer used. The name is described as something hidden, buried, and preserved in memory.

**Interpretive function:**  
This is the central passage for the case. The Model Reader must infer that the narrator has an original personal name, but that the text deliberately withholds it. The absence of the name becomes part of the meaning of the narrative.

**Function in the ontology:**  
This passage can be modeled as the main cue for the presupposition that Offred's true name exists but remains textually unavailable.

## Passage 3: Historical Notes and patronymic naming

**Source:** Margaret Atwood, *The Handmaid's Tale*, PDF p. 294.

**Micro-quote:**  
> "Offred gives no clue"

**Working passage:**  
> "She does not see fit to supply us with her original name, and indeed all official records of it would have been destroyed upon her entry into the Rachel and Leah Re-education Centre. “Offred” gives no clue, since, like “Ofglen” and “Ofwarren,” it was a patronymic, composed of the possessive preposition and the first name of the gentleman in question. Such names were taken by these women upon their entry into a connection with the household of a specific Commander, and relinquished by them upon leaving it."

**Contextual paraphrase:**  
In the Historical Notes, Professor Pieixoto explains that the narrator does not provide her original name. He also explains that names such as "Offred", "Ofglen", and "Ofwarren" are patronymic names formed through a possessive structure linked to the Commander of each household.

**Interpretive function:**  
This passage retrospectively reframes "Offred" as an institutional name rather than a personal one. It confirms that the name encodes belonging or possession, while also confirming that the narrator's original name remains unresolved.

**Function in the ontology:**  
This passage can be modeled as a later textual cue that activates knowledge about patronymic naming, possession, and institutional identity.

## Interpretive issue

This case requires the Model Reader to infer that:

1. "Offred" is not the narrator's original name.
2. The narrator has another personal name, but the text withholds it.
3. Personal names survive as traces of identity within Gilead's system.
4. Gilead replaces personal identity with institutional naming.
5. The name "Offred" encodes possession or belonging.
6. The Historical Notes confirm the structure of the name but do not recover the narrator's original identity.

## Important interpretive caution

The list of names on PDF p. 14 makes "June" a possible candidate, but the text does not explicitly confirm that June is the narrator's original name. Therefore, this should be treated only as a possible or alternative interpretation, not as a fully licensed interpretation.

The name "Offred" requires interpretive cooperation because its meaning is not exhausted by its surface form.
