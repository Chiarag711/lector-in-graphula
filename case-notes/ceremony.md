# Case: The Ceremony

## Case status
Decision:
- [x] Keep
- [ ] Replace
- [ ] Modify

This case focuses on the Ceremony as a ritualized reproductive act in *The Handmaid's Tale*. The selected passages show how Gilead frames the act through biblical quotation, prayer, and institutional language, while Offred's narration forces the reader to recognize the inadequacy of ordinary sexual and romantic categories.

The case is based on four textual moments:
1. The biblical justification of reproduction through the stories of Rachel, Leah, and Bilhah.
2. The ritual closure through silent prayer and blessing.
3. Offred's description of the act and her rejection of terms such as making love, copulating, and rape.
4. Offred's explicit denial of passion, love, romance, desire, arousal, and pleasure.

## Overview

The Ceremony is one of the clearest examples of interpretive cooperation in the novel. Gilead presents the act as a religiously justified reproductive ritual, but Offred's narration exposes the violence, bodily dissociation, and constrained agency hidden behind that institutional frame.

The Model Reader must therefore coordinate multiple interpretive resources. Biblical knowledge is required to understand why the story of Rachel, Leah, and Bilhah is used as justification. Knowledge of ritual and prayer is required to understand how the act is sacralized. Ordinary sexual and ethical codes are also activated, but they fail to classify the event adequately. The result is a licensed interpretation of the Ceremony as an institutionalized reproductive ritual that is religiously framed and ethically coercive.

## Passage 1: Biblical justification

**Source:** Margaret Atwood, *The Handmaid's Tale*, PDF p. 91.

**Micro-quote:**
> "Give me children, or else I die"

**Working passage:**
> "It’s the usual story, the usual stories. God to Adam, God to Noah. Be fruitful, and multiply, and replenish the earth. Then comes the mouldy old Rachel and Leah stuff we had drummed into us at the Centre. Give me children, or else I die. Am I in God’s stead, who hath withheld from thee the fruit of the womb? Behold my maid Bilhah. She shall bear upon my knees, that I may also have children by her. And so on and so forth."

**Contextual paraphrase:**
The Commander reads biblical passages before the Ceremony. The selected story links fertility, reproduction, and female service to a scriptural narrative. Offred recognizes that this passage has already been repeatedly imposed on the Handmaids at the Centre.

**Interpretive function:**
This passage activates the religious and reproductive frame used by Gilead to justify the Ceremony. The Model Reader must understand that Gilead does not present the act as private sexuality, but as obedience to a scriptural model.

**Function in the ontology:**
This passage can be modeled as a textual fragment that contains a biblical cue. The cue activates a religious frame, a reproductive subcode, and intrinsic knowledge about biblical justification. It supports the inference that the Ceremony is institutionally framed as a sacred reproductive duty.

## Passage 2: Ritual prayer

**Source:** Margaret Atwood, *The Handmaid's Tale*, PDF p. 93.

**Micro-quote:**
> "a moment of silent prayer"

**Working passage:**
> "The Commander opens his eyes, notices, frowns, ceases to notice. ‘Now we will have a moment of silent prayer,’ says the Commander. ‘We will ask for a blessing, and for success in all our ventures.’"

**Contextual paraphrase:**
After the biblical reading, the Commander calls for silent prayer and asks for blessing and success. The Ceremony is therefore embedded in a sequence of ritual actions: reading, prayer, blessing, and obedience.

**Interpretive function:**
This passage confirms that the Ceremony is not only justified by biblical language but also performed as a ritual procedure. The Model Reader must recognize that prayer transforms the act into a formalized institutional ceremony.

**Function in the ontology:**
This passage can be modeled as a textual fragment that contains a ritual-prayer cue. The cue activates a religious ritual frame and supports the inference that Gilead sacralizes the reproductive act.

## Passage 3: Category failure during the act

**Source:** Margaret Atwood, *The Handmaid's Tale*, PDF pp. 96–97.

**Micro-quote:**
> "only one is involved"

**Working passage:**
> "My red skirt is hitched up to my waist, though no higher. Below it the Commander is fucking. What he is fucking is the lower part of my body. I do not say making love, because this is not what he’s doing. Copulating too would be inaccurate, because it would imply two people and only one is involved. Nor does rape cover it: nothing is going on here that I haven’t signed up for. There wasn’t a lot of choice but there was some, and this is what I chose."

**Contextual paraphrase:**
Offred describes the act while refusing several available categories. It is not making love, because there is no intimacy. It is not simply copulating, because that would imply reciprocal participation. Even the term rape is presented as insufficient, because the situation involves coercion within a system where Offred has been forced to choose among limited options.

**Interpretive function:**
This passage is central because it stages an interpretive problem. The Model Reader is led through a series of inadequate labels and must infer that the Ceremony exceeds ordinary sexual terminology. The act must be interpreted as institutionalized reproductive coercion rather than as ordinary sex.

**Function in the ontology:**
This passage can be modeled as a textual fragment that contains a category-failure cue. The cue activates ordinary sexual subcodes, ethical knowledge about consent and coercion, and knowledge about constrained choice. It supports the inference that the Ceremony cannot be classified through ordinary categories such as love, reciprocal sex, or individual sexual violence alone.

## Passage 4: Rejection of passion, love, and desire

**Source:** Margaret Atwood, *The Handmaid's Tale*, PDF p. 97.

**Micro-quote:**
> "It has nothing to do with passion"

**Working passage:**
> "What’s going on in this room, under Serena Joy’s silvery canopy, is not exciting. It has nothing to do with passion or love or romance or any of those other notions we used to titillate ourselves with. It has nothing to do with sexual desire, at least for me, and certainly not for Serena. Arousal and orgasm are no longer thought necessary; they would be a symptom of frivolity merely, like jazz garters or beauty spots: superfluous distractions for the light-minded. Outdated."

**Contextual paraphrase:**
Offred explicitly excludes passion, love, romance, desire, arousal, and pleasure from the Ceremony. These ordinary categories are marked as irrelevant or obsolete within Gilead's reproductive order.

**Interpretive function:**
This passage strengthens the conflict between ordinary sexual codes and Gilead's reproductive-ritual code. The Model Reader must infer that the Ceremony is designed to remove sexuality from the sphere of intimacy, pleasure, and mutual desire.

**Function in the ontology:**
This passage can be modeled as a textual fragment that contains a passion-negation cue. The cue activates ordinary sexual and romantic subcodes only to negate them. It supports the inference that Gilead redefines sexuality as reproductive duty and suppresses pleasure as frivolous or ideologically dangerous.

## Interpretive issue

This case requires the Model Reader to infer that:
1. Gilead justifies the Ceremony through biblical and reproductive narratives.
2. The Ceremony is performed within a ritual frame made of reading, prayer, and blessing.
3. Ordinary sexual categories such as love, romance, desire, and pleasure do not apply.
4. Available terms such as making love, copulating, and rape are shown to be interpretively unstable or incomplete.
5. The Ceremony is therefore best understood as a religiously framed, institutionalized reproductive ritual.
6. Offred's narration exposes the coercive structure hidden behind Gilead's sacred language.

## Important interpretive caution

The ontology should not reduce the Ceremony to a single ordinary category such as sex, rape, or religious ritual. The point of the case is that the text forces the reader to negotiate among several inadequate codes. The licensed interpretation should preserve this complexity: the Ceremony is ritualized, reproductive, institutional, coercive, and religiously justified.

## Summary

The Ceremony case shows how the text constructs a Model Reader capable of recognizing the conflict between Gilead's official religious-reproductive code and ordinary sexual, romantic, and ethical codes. Biblical quotation and prayer frame the Ceremony as sacred duty, while Offred's narration reveals that the act cannot be understood as love, desire, reciprocal sex, or simple individual choice. The reader must therefore actualize a more complex interpretation: the Ceremony is an institutionalized reproductive ritual that uses religious language to legitimize coercion.
