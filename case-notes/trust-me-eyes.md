# Case: "Trust me" ambiguity case

## Case status

Decision:

This case focuses on the ambiguity of Nick's role and trustworthiness in *The Handmaid's Tale*. It shows how the Model Reader reconstructs implicit meaning through textual cues, intrinsic knowledge, presuppositions, predictions, probability disjunctions, and inferences.

The case is based on four textual moments:

1. An early moment in which Offred suspects that Nick may be an Eye.
2. The final scene in which Nick asks Offred to trust him and claims that the men are connected to Mayday.
3. The ambiguous arrival of the men, whose authority may signal either arrest or rescue.
4. The unresolved final movement into the van, described as either darkness or light.

---

## Overview

In *The Handmaid's Tale*, Nick's position remains uncertain for both Offred and the reader. Earlier in the novel, Offred considers the possibility that Nick may be an Eye, a member of Gilead's surveillance apparatus. Near the end, when a black van arrives, Nick tells her that the men are connected to Mayday and asks her to trust him. However, the situation does not fully resolve the earlier suspicion: the van, the men, the reference to state secrets, and the lack of clear authorization all continue to support more than one possible interpretation.

This case is useful for our ontology because it foregrounds Eco's idea of interpretive cooperation as a process of prediction and revision. The Model Reader must connect earlier cues to later ones, activate knowledge about surveillance, resistance networks, betrayal, and trust, and hold open competing possible outcomes. The text does not simply state whether Nick is a rescuer or a threat; it organizes cues that make both possibilities interpretively available.

## Passage 1: Early suspicion of Nick

**Source:** Margaret Atwood, *The Handmaid's Tale*, PDF pp. 26–27.

**Micro-quote:**
> "Perhaps he is an Eye"

**Working passage:**
> He looks at me, and sees me looking. [...] I drop my head and turn so that the white wings hide my face, and keep walking. He’s just taken a risk, but for what? What if I were to report him? Perhaps he was merely being friendly. Perhaps he saw the look on my face and mistook it for something else. Really what I wanted was the cigarette. Perhaps it was a test, to see what I would do. Perhaps he is an Eye.

**Contextual paraphrase:**
Offred notices Nick looking at her and considers several possible explanations for his behavior. His gesture may be friendly, flirtatious, careless, or strategic. The passage ends by introducing the possibility that he may be an Eye, transforming an apparently minor interaction into a sign of surveillance and danger.

**Interpretive function:**
This passage establishes Nick as an ambiguous figure. The Model Reader must infer that a small social gesture can function as a test within a surveillance regime. The cue requires knowledge of Gilead's system of informants and of the risks attached to apparently ordinary interactions. It opens a predictive branch: Nick may be a possible ally, or he may be an agent of control.

**Function in the ontology:**
This passage can be modeled as a textual fragment that activates surveillance knowledge, suspicion, and the presupposition that apparently friendly behavior may conceal institutional monitoring. It supports an inference that Nick's identity and loyalty are uncertain, and it opens a probability disjunction between threat and alliance.

## Passage 2: Nick's request for trust

**Source:** Margaret Atwood, *The Handmaid's Tale*, PDF pp. 283–285.

**Micro-quote:**
> "Trust me"

**Working passage:**
> As I’m standing up I hear the black van. [...] It turns into the driveway, stops. [...] Two men detach themselves from the shape of it, come up the front steps, ring the bell. [...] I expect a stranger, but it’s Nick who pushes open the door, flicks on the light. I can’t place that, unless he’s one of them. There was always that possibility. Nick, the private Eye. Dirty work is done by dirty people. You shit, I think. I open my mouth to say it, but he comes over, close to me, whispers. “It’s all right. It’s Mayday. Go with them.” He calls me by my real name. Why should this mean anything? “Them?” I say. I see the two men standing behind him, the overhead light in the hallway making skulls of their heads. “You must be crazy.” My suspicion hovers in the air above him, a dark angel warning me away. I can almost see it. Why shouldn’t he know about Mayday? All the Eyes must know about it; they’ll have squeezed it, crushed it, twisted it out of enough bodies, enough mouths by now. “Trust me,” he says; which in itself has never been a talisman, carries no guarantee. But I snatch at it, this offer. It’s all I’m left with.

**Contextual paraphrase:**
When the black van arrives, Offred expects danger. Nick appears and tells her that the men are connected to Mayday, but the information does not remove her suspicion. His knowledge of Mayday and of her real name may indicate trust and intimacy, but it may also be compatible with the knowledge possessed by the Eyes. Offred accepts his words not because they prove safety, but because she has no better option.

**Interpretive function:**
This passage intensifies the ambiguity introduced earlier. The phrase "Trust me" functions as a cue, but not as proof. The Model Reader must activate knowledge about clandestine resistance, surveillance, torture, betrayal, and the fragility of signs under authoritarian conditions. The passage supports two competing predictions: Nick may be helping Offred escape through Mayday, or he may be participating in her arrest.

**Function in the ontology:**
This passage can be modeled as a textual fragment that activates trust knowledge, surveillance knowledge, resistance knowledge, and constrained-choice knowledge. It supports inferences about Nick's ambiguous role and generates alternative predictions about rescue and betrayal.

## Passage 3: Ambiguous authorization of the arrest

**Source:** Margaret Atwood, *The Handmaid's Tale*, PDF pp. 283–285.

**Micro-quote:**
> "Violation of state secrets"

**Working passage:**
> Serena Joy stands in the hallway, under the mirror, looking up, incredulous. The Commander is behind her, the sitting-room door is open. [...] “What has she done?” says Serena Joy. She wasn’t the one who called them, then. Whatever she had in store for me, it was more private. “We can’t say, Ma’am,” says the one in front of me. “Sorry.” “I need to see your authorization,” says the Commander. “You have a warrant?” I could scream now, cling to the banister, relinquish dignity. I could stop them, at least for a moment. If they’re real they’ll stay, if not they’ll run away. Leaving me here. “Not that we need one, Sir, but all is in order,” says the first one again. “Violation of state secrets.” [...] "Bitch," she says. "After all he did for you."

**Contextual paraphrase:**
The men who come for Offred present themselves as officials, but the scene remains uncertain. Serena Joy appears not to have called them, and the Commander asks for authorization. The claim of "state secrets" sounds official, but it does not fully prove that the men are genuine agents of Gilead. Offred considers testing them by resisting, but doing so could expose her if the situation is actually a rescue.

**Interpretive function:**
This passage keeps the probability disjunction open. The Model Reader must infer that official language and procedure can be signs of real state authority, but they can also be performed or imitated. The passage does not allow a simple conclusion. Instead, it forces the reader to evaluate the available cues while recognizing that each cue can support more than one interpretation.

**Function in the ontology:**
This passage can be modeled as a textual fragment that activates institutional-authority knowledge, warrant or authorization knowledge, and knowledge about clandestine deception. It supports an inference that the status of the men remains ambiguous and contributes to alternative interpretations of the scene as either arrest or rescue.

## Passage 4: Darkness or light

**Source:** Margaret Atwood, *The Handmaid's Tale*, PDF pp. 283–285.

**Micro-quote:**
> "darkness within; or else the light"

**Working passage:**
> The van waits in the driveway, its double doors stand open. The two of them, one on either side now, take me by the elbows to help me in. Whether this is my end or a new beginning I have no way of knowing: I have given myself over into the hands of strangers, because it can’t be helped. And so I step up, into the darkness within; or else the light.

**Contextual paraphrase:**
Offred enters the van without knowing whether she is being taken to death, punishment, escape, or freedom. The final contrast between darkness and light preserves the uncertainty instead of resolving it. The movement into the van becomes both a possible ending and a possible beginning.

**Interpretive function:**
This passage explicitly withholds interpretive closure. The Model Reader must understand that the text licenses competing outcomes without confirming one inside the narrated scene. The final cue does not eliminate ambiguity; it formalizes it as part of the ending's meaning.

**Function in the ontology:**
This passage can be modeled as a textual fragment that activates knowledge about open endings, uncertainty, and alternative possible outcomes. It supports an inference that the narrative closes on a probability disjunction between rescue and destruction.

## Interpretive issue

This case requires the Model Reader to infer that:

1. Nick's early behavior can be interpreted both as ordinary human contact and as a possible surveillance test.
2. The phrase "Trust me" does not function as reliable proof, because trust is unstable within Gilead's regime of surveillance and coercion.
3. The arrival of the black van and the claim of state authority can support both the interpretation of arrest and the interpretation of rescue.
4. Nick's reference to Mayday and Offred's real name supports a rescue hypothesis, but does not completely eliminate suspicion.
5. The final movement into the van preserves a probability disjunction between ending and beginning, darkness and light.
6. The main meaning of the case is not simply that Nick is good or bad, but that the text constructs his role as an object of interpretive uncertainty.

---

## Important interpretive caution

The knowledge graph should not assert as explicit fact that Nick certainly rescues Offred, nor that he is certainly an Eye. The text licenses a structured ambiguity. The rescue interpretation is strongly supported by Nick's reference to Mayday and Offred's real name, but the suspicion of betrayal remains textually active in the scene.

This case should therefore not be reduced to a plot summary. It should be modeled as an interpretive operation in which textual cues generate competing predictions and alternative interpretations. The ontology should represent the Model Reader as a textual strategy, not as an empirical reader deciding personally whether to trust Nick.

## Summary

This case shows how *The Handmaid's Tale* constructs Nick's role through ambiguity rather than direct explanation. Early suspicion that Nick may be an Eye returns during the final scene, when he tells Offred to trust him and claims that the men have come from Mayday. The Model Reader must connect scattered cues, activate knowledge about surveillance and resistance, and evaluate competing predictions without receiving full confirmation.

Through these cues, the Model Reader infers that Offred's final departure is deliberately suspended between rescue and destruction. The main licensed interpretation is that the text constructs trust as an uncertain interpretive act under conditions of surveillance and coercion.
