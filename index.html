Lector in Graphula

Modeling Eco’s “Lettore Modello” and Intrinsic Knowledge in The Handmaid’s Tale

A Semantic Web project that formalizes selected aspects of Umberto Eco’s theory of interpretive cooperation and instantiates them as a Knowledge Graph for four interpretive cases from Margaret Atwood’s novel.

01

Overview

The project

Abstract

Lector in Graphula was created with the aim of translating the complexity of Umberto Eco’s theory of interpretative cooperation into a formal and computational model, harnessing the potential of Semantic Web technologies. The project was developed as part of the Knowledge Representation and Extraction course, taught by Professor Aldo Gangemi during the 2025/2026 academic year, within the Master’s Degree programme in Digital Humanities and Digital Knowledge at the University of Bologna.

The conceptual framework of the project is based entirely on Eco’s semiotic theory, formalising within an ontology the implicit processes that guide reading. The model maps and standardises the logical relationships between fundamental elements such as textual cues, the reader’s intrinsic knowledge, presuppositions, inferences, predictions and interpretations.

Through the development of this semantic framework, the reader’s cooperative activity is translated into a structured Knowledge Graph, the validation and practical application of which were carried out on a selection of extracts from Margaret Atwood’s novel The Handmaid’s Tale.

Research question

How can Eco’s theory of the Model Reader be formalized through an ontology and instantiated as a Knowledge Graph in order to represent the intrinsic interpretive knowledge required to understand selected excerpts from The Handmaid’s Tale?

Why The Handmaid’s Tale?

The Handmaid’s Tale strongly depends on implicit and intrinsic knowledge. Gilead’s rules, hierarchies, rituals and ideological mechanisms are not explained all at once. They are gradually reconstructed through textual cues, omissions, repetitions and ambiguities.

The novel presupposes a reader capable of activating biblical references, dystopian conventions, knowledge about gender and power relations, institutional language, social classification systems and historical allusions.

The project does not model the complete plot. It represents how selected passages guide a reader from the textual surface towards an interpretation through structured inferential processes.

The project does not model the entire novel, establish one definitive interpretation or claim to automate literary interpretation. It makes selected interpretive chains explicit, inspectable and queryable.

02

Theoretical framework

The Model Reader and the “lazy machine”

The text as a lazy machine

In Eco’s theory, a text does not state everything required for its own interpretation. It contains gaps and unsaid elements that presuppose the cooperation of a competent reader.

This textual “laziness” is not a defect. It is the condition that makes interpretation possible. The reader activates knowledge, codes, frames and inferential operations to actualize what the text leaves implicit.

Interpretive cooperation

Interpretation is understood as a constrained process rather than as unrestricted subjectivity. The reader moves from the linear manifestation of the text to its semantic actualization by activating interpretive resources, producing inferences and projecting possible developments.

lig:ModelAuthor

Model Author

The textual strategy that organizes instructions, cues, gaps and interpretive constraints.

lig:ModelReader

Model Reader

The textual strategy and set of competencies presupposed and constructed by the text.

lig:Encyclopedia

Encyclopedia

The network of background knowledge from which relevant interpretive resources are activated.

lig:TextualCue

Textual cue

A locally identifiable textual element that activates an interpretive process.

lig:IntrinsicKnowledge

Intrinsic knowledge

Knowledge required for interpretation but not fully expressed in the linear text.

lig:InferentialWalk

Inferential walk

An interpretive hypothesis or projected possible world that may later be confirmed, rejected or left open.

Critical distinction

Model Reader ≠ Empirical Reader

Model Author ≠ Empirical Author

Model Reader and Model Author are textual strategies. They are not Umberto Eco, Margaret Atwood or individual real readers.

03

Research process

Methodology

The project followed a selective and iterative workflow, moving from theoretical analysis to ontology design, Knowledge Graph construction and formal evaluation.

01

Theoretical selection

Selected concepts from Eco’s theory of interpretive cooperation were identified and delimited.

  • Model Reader and Model Author
  • Textual cues and textual cooperation
  • Encyclopedia, codes and frames
  • Presuppositions and inferences
  • Predictions and probability disjunctions

02

Case-study selection

Fifteen textual moments were selected and grouped into four connected interpretive cases. Each moment contributes a distinct cue or interpretive function.

  • Offred’s name: 3 moments
  • The Ceremony: 4 moments
  • Nick and the Eyes: 4 moments
  • Color-coded clothing: 4 moments

03

Ontology design

Competency Questions and modeling requirements guided the construction of the ontology in Protégé. Classes, properties, domains, ranges, restrictions and disjointness axioms were encoded in OWL 2 and serialized in Turtle.

04

Knowledge Graph construction

The four cases were manually instantiated as an ABox. Each case connects several textual fragments and cues with the interpretive resources and operations required to reach one or more interpretations.

05

Materialization and evaluation

SWRL rules were used to materialize selected relations across the interpretive chains. SPARQL queries were then used to answer eight Competency Questions and three Negative Competency Questions.

06

External comparison

Selected textual cues were processed through POLANYI++ to compare its implicit-knowledge outputs with the manually constructed Knowledge Graph.

The comparison was exploratory and was not used as an automatic validator.

Modeling principle

The objective was not to reproduce every possible reading of the novel, but to make a limited set of textually motivated interpretive paths explicit, inspectable and queryable.

Open-world considerations

Under OWL’s open-world semantics, the absence of evidence does not imply that a statement is false. Consequently, a weak or unsupported interpretation does not necessarily produce a logical inconsistency.

Explicit OWL constraints were used only where a formal incompatibility could be represented. Broader methodological checks were performed through SPARQL queries and Negative Competency Questions.

  • ModelAuthor is disjoint with EmpiricalAuthor.
  • Interpretation is disjoint with Use.

  • Code is disjoint with Frame.

05

Knowledge Graph

Four interpretive cases

Each case combines all the textual moments selected during close reading. The final path is a synthesis of several fragments, not the result of a single isolated quotation.

Case 01 Offred’s name Identity, prohibition and institutional naming Open

The meaning of “Offred” emerges gradually. Three separate fragments establish that personal names survive in secret, that the narrator has another forbidden name, and that “Offred” is an institutional patronymic linked to household assignment and possession.

Open the complete case notes

Passage 01 · PDF p. 14

Secret exchange of names

“we exchanged names”

At the Red Centre, the Handmaids secretly pass their personal names from bed to bed. The act presents names as forbidden but still preserved traces of identity.

Interpretive function

Prepares the reader to distinguish personal identity from the institutional names later imposed by Gilead.

Passage 02 · PDF p. 87

The hidden real name

“My name isn’t Offred”

Offred states that she has another name that is forbidden and no longer used. She preserves it privately as a memory and a trace of continuity with her former self.

Interpretive function

Triggers the presupposition that the real name exists within the fictional world even though the text deliberately withholds it.

Passage 03 · PDF p. 294

Historical Notes and patronymic naming

“Offred gives no clue”

The Historical Notes explain that names such as Offred, Ofglen and Ofwarren are patronymics formed through a possessive structure and tied to a specific Commander.

Interpretive function

Retrospectively confirms that “Offred” is institutional, replaceable and structured by belonging rather than personal identity.

Interpretive synthesis

Several cues converge on one broader interpretation

Textual fragments Three connected passages
Intrinsic knowledge HiddenRealNameKnowledge
Presupposition HiddenNamePresupposition
Inference OffredIdentityInference
Interpretation GileadErasesFemaleIdentity

The reader reconstructs “Offred” as an imposed name of possession and understands the continued absence of the narrator’s personal name as narratively meaningful.

Interpretive caution. The list of names at the Red Centre makes “June” a possible candidate, but the text does not explicitly confirm it as Offred’s original name. It must therefore remain an alternative interpretation rather than a confirmed fact.
Case 02 The Ceremony Ritual, coercion and category inadequacy Open

Four passages construct the Ceremony through biblical justification, ritual prayer, the failure of ordinary sexual categories and the explicit exclusion of passion and desire. The interpretation depends on the conflict among several codes rather than on one label.

Open the complete case notes

Passage 01 · PDF p. 91

Biblical justification

“Give me children, or else I die”

The Commander reads the stories of Rachel, Leah and Bilhah before the Ceremony. Reproduction is presented through a scriptural model already imposed on the Handmaids at the Centre.

Interpretive function

Activates biblical knowledge and a religious-reproductive frame that presents the act as sacred duty.

Passage 02 · PDF p. 93

Ritual prayer

“a moment of silent prayer”

The Commander calls for prayer, blessing and success. Reading, prayer and obedience turn the act into a formal institutional procedure.

Interpretive function

Confirms that the Ceremony is performed inside a ritual frame and not represented as private sexuality.

Passage 03 · PDF pp. 96–97

Category failure during the act

“only one is involved”

Offred rejects “making love” and “copulating” as inaccurate, while also presenting “rape” as insufficient to capture coercion structured through severely limited choice.

Interpretive function

Forces the Model Reader to negotiate among inadequate categories and infer an institutional form of reproductive coercion.

Passage 04 · PDF p. 97

Rejection of passion and desire

“It has nothing to do with passion”

Passion, love, romance, desire, arousal and pleasure are explicitly excluded from the Ceremony and treated as obsolete distractions within Gilead’s reproductive order.

Interpretive function

Strengthens the conflict between ordinary sexual and romantic subcodes and Gilead’s reproductive-ritual code.

Interpretive synthesis

Religious framing and category failure converge

Textual fragments Four connected passages
Frame CeremonyRitualFrame
Intrinsic knowledge ConstrainedChoiceKnowledge
Inference CeremonyCategoryFailureInference
Interpretation CeremonyAsInstitutionalizedReproductiveRitual

The Ceremony is interpreted as a religiously framed, institutionalized reproductive ritual whose sacred language legitimizes coercion and removes sexuality from intimacy, reciprocity and pleasure.

Interpretive caution. The case should not reduce the Ceremony to one ordinary category such as sex, rape or ritual. Its meaning lies partly in the text’s demonstration that each of these categories is incomplete on its own.
Case 03 Nick and the Eyes Surveillance, mistrust and competing predictions Open

Nick’s role remains uncertain across four moments. An early suspicion is reactivated when the black van arrives; his request for trust, the men’s ambiguous authority and the unresolved ending support both arrest and rescue predictions.

Open the complete case notes

Passage 01 · PDF pp. 26–27

Early suspicion of Nick

“Perhaps he is an Eye”

Offred considers several explanations for Nick’s gesture: friendliness, flirtation, a test or surveillance. A minor social interaction becomes potentially dangerous.

Interpretive function

Activates surveillance knowledge and opens an initial branch between possible alliance and institutional threat.

Passage 02 · PDF pp. 283–285

Nick’s request for trust

“Trust me”

Nick claims that the men are connected to Mayday and uses Offred’s real name. These signs support rescue, but they are also compatible with knowledge that the Eyes might possess.

Interpretive function

Intensifies rather than resolves ambiguity: trust functions as an offer without becoming proof.

Passage 03 · PDF pp. 283–285

Ambiguous authorization

“Violation of state secrets”

The men use official language, but the Commander asks for authorization and Serena Joy appears not to have called them. State procedure may be genuine or performed.

Interpretive function

Keeps the probability disjunction open by making each sign of authority compatible with both arrest and clandestine rescue.

Passage 04 · PDF pp. 283–285

Darkness or light

“darkness within; or else the light”

Offred enters the van without knowing whether the movement marks punishment, death, escape or a new beginning. The final contrast preserves uncertainty.

Interpretive function

Withholds closure and licenses competing outcomes without confirming either inside the narrated scene.

Interpretive synthesis

The case culminates in a probability disjunction

Probability disjunction FinalOutcomeProbabilityDisjunction

The Knowledge Graph preserves ambiguity as a structured interpretive outcome rather than collapsing Nick into a certain ally or enemy.

Interpretive caution. The graph should assert neither that Nick certainly rescues Offred nor that he is certainly an Eye. The text licenses a structured uncertainty.
Case 04 Color-coded clothing Visual classification and identity control Open

Four moments allow the Model Reader to reconstruct a society-wide chromatic role/status code. The first three build the internal code of Gilead; the Historical Notes add a second interpretive level and a conjectural historical origin for the Handmaids’ red.

Open the complete case notes

Passage 01 · PDF pp. 17–18

Red “defines us”

“the colour of blood, which defines us”

Offred describes prescribed red garments, white wings and color-assigned umbrellas. Red, blue, black and green already correlate household positions with visible signs.

Interpretive function

Activates knowledge about uniforms and institutional dress, supporting the inference that color is assigned rather than incidental.

Passage 02 · PDF pp. 32–33

Social enumeration

“These women are not divided into functions”

The street scene enumerates Handmaids in red, Marthas in green, Econowives in multicolored stripes and widows in black. The striped dress combines several functions.

Interpretive function

Makes the code compositional and links chromatic classification to gender, function and household rank.

Passage 03 · PDF p. 89

The household as visual inventory

“as if taking inventory”

Before the Ceremony, people are described by position and color: one in red, one in blue, two in green and a man in black. Personal names become unnecessary.

Interpretive function

Presupposes that the reader has already learned the code and supports the interpretation that social function substitutes for individual identity.

Passage 04 · PDF p. 295

Historical reframing of the red

“the suggestion that the Handmaids wear red”

In the Historical Notes, Pieixoto attributes costume design to Waterford and presents the P.O.W.-uniform origin of the red as a second-hand, hedged claim.

Interpretive function

Creates a second Model Reader level and reinterprets Gilead’s apparently natural symbol as a deliberately designed, historically derivative artifact.

Interpretive synthesis

Internal code and historical reframing remain distinct

Model Reader level 1

Internal chromatic code
Distributed clothing cues → GileadChromaticCodeControlsSocialIdentity

Repeated and convergent evidence licenses the interpretation that Gilead uses color to make roles and hierarchy visually legible.

Model Reader level 2

Historical reframing
Historical Notes cue → POWUniformOriginConjecture

The later attribution activates extra-textual historical knowledge but remains a conjecture inside the fiction.

Interpretive caution. The internal chromatic code is supported by repeated textual evidence. The proposed P.O.W. origin is hedged, second-hand and introduced by a character in the pseudo-academic frame; it must not be represented as a direct narrative fact.

SWRL materialization

Interpretive-chain rules

Three SWRL rules materialize relations across the interpretive chains. They were tested through Drools in Protégé.

Grounding inferences

containsCue(?fragment, ?cue)
^ supportsInference(?cue, ?inference)
→ groundsInference(?fragment, ?inference)

Produced 28 groundsInference assertions.

Contributing to interpretations

supportsInference(?cue, ?inference)
^ licensesInterpretation(?inference, ?interpretation)
→ contributesToInterpretation(?cue, ?interpretation)

Produced 29 contributesToInterpretation assertions.

Supporting inferences with knowledge

requiresIntrinsicKnowledge(?cue, ?knowledge)
^ supportsInference(?cue, ?inference)
→ knowledgeSupportsInference(?knowledge, ?inference)

Produced 48 knowledgeSupportsInference assertions.

07

External comparison

POLANYI++

POLANYI++ was used as an external comparison tool for inspecting implicit knowledge in selected textual cues.

Its outputs were reviewed and compared with the IntrinsicKnowledge, Presupposition, Inference, Prediction and Interpretation individuals manually modeled in the Knowledge Graph.

POLANYI++ is not part of the official Knowledge Graph and is not used as an automatic validation mechanism.

01 Offred’s name “My name isn’t Offred”

EXTRACT · m1 TACIT · EXPLAIN · b4 PRESUPPOSITION · b5 IMPLICATURE · b12 PERSPECTIVE

POLANYI++ identifies a contrast between an imposed institutional name and a hidden personal name. The output overlaps with the Knowledge Graph’s representation of memory, identity suppression and residual personal agency.

02 The Ceremony Category failure and coercion

The EXTRACT step produced a Turtle graph without a narrative synthesis. A DISCOVER step was therefore applied to the generated graph.

The synthesis characterized the Ceremony through objectification and lack of agency. These observations overlap with the Knowledge Graph’s representation of constrained choice and institutionalized reproductive coercion.

03 Nick and the Eyes Suspicion and competing possibilities

EXTRACT · m1 TACIT · EXPLAIN · b4 PRESUPPOSITION · b5 IMPLICATURE · b10 THEORY OF MIND · b12 PERSPECTIVE · b13 HYPOTHETICAL · b26 LACUNA

POLANYI++ identifies an ambiguous interaction filtered through a suspicious and self-protective perspective. The Knowledge Graph formalizes this uncertainty through predictions, probability disjunctions and alternative interpretations.

04 Color-coded clothing Social signs and institutional dress

POLANYI++ generated a Turtle graph but no human-readable synthesis. The graph was interpreted manually for the methodological comparison.

The output suggests implicit assumptions about color as a social marker, institutional dress, visible classification and identity control.
