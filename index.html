§ 01

About the project

Lector in Graphula, an ontology of Umberto Eco's theory of the “Lettore Modello”, was developed within the course Knowledge Representation and Extraction, taught by Professor Aldo Gangemi during a.y. 2025/2026 of the Master's Degree in Digital Humanities and Digital Knowledge (DHDK) at the University of Bologna. The project's aim is to use Semantic Web technologies to model the implicit interpretive processes that occur while reading a narrative text.

The project adopts Umberto Eco's interpretive cooperation as its theoretical model, as outlined in Lector in fabula, specifically the notion of the “Lettore Modello”, understood as a textual strategy that activates encyclopedic knowledge, inferences, frames, and intrinsic knowledge. The ontology formalizes the main concepts of Eco's theory, while the knowledge graph instantiates them on selected excerpts from The Handmaid's Tale. The POLANYI++ tool is used as an external comparison for the intrinsic knowledge manually modeled according to Eco's categories.

A text is a fabric of blank spaces, of the unsaid, which presupposes that someone will fill them in: this “laziness” is not a flaw, it is the very condition under which narrative texts function. — The central thesis: the text as a Lazy Machine

Project goal

To demonstrate how knowledge graphs and Semantic Web technologies can encode, analyze, and formally verify the implicit cognitive processes that occur during the reading of a text, taking as a model Umberto Eco's theory of interpretive cooperation, applied to the case study The Handmaid's Tale.

GOAL 1

Making the “Lazy Machine” computable

A text does not say everything explicitly: it relies on “blank spaces” that the reader must fill in. The goal is to translate Eco's semiotic intuitions about this filling-in of intrinsic meaning into structured OWL logical constraints.

GOAL 2

Modeling agents and strategies (TBox)

An ontology built from scratch, in which the Model Author imposes roles and constraints, the Empirical Reader is called to satisfy the role of Model Reader, and the Encyclopedia provides the background knowledge the reader draws on.

GOAL 3

Validating the limits of interpretation (ABox & SPARQL)

Populating the ontology with stories drawn from the novel and showing, through SPARQL queries, that a coherent interpretation can be distinguished from an arbitrary use of the text.

§ 02

Why The Handmaid's Tale?

The Handmaid's Tale (Margaret Atwood, 1985) was chosen because it is a narrative text that strongly depends on implicit and intrinsic knowledge. The world of Gilead is not explained all at once: its rules, social hierarchies, rituals, and ideological mechanisms are gradually reconstructed by the reader through textual cues, omissions, repetitions, and ambiguities.

Atwood's text requires a Model Reader capable of activating different forms of encyclopedic knowledge: biblical references, dystopian conventions, gender and power relations, institutional language, social classification systems, and historical allusions. Meaning is produced not only by what the text explicitly states, but also by what it presupposes and asks the reader to infer.

For this reason, the novel functions as a practical testing ground for the ontology. Eco's theory provides the conceptual schema; Atwood's novel provides concrete instances that populate the knowledge graph. The aim is not to model the entire plot, but to represent how four selected interpretive cases guide the reader from textual surface to interpretation through structured inferential processes.

§ 03

User scenario

A student of narrative theory is reading The Handmaid's Tale and finds the sentence “the colour of blood, which defines us”. The text never states Gilead's dress code explicitly. What must a reader already know, presuppose, and infer to understand it?

Lector in Graphula makes this implicit work explicit and queryable. Starting from the textual cue, the student retrieves the full interpretive chain: the intrinsic knowledge it requires, the presupposition it triggers, the inferences it supports, and the interpretation it licenses, each marked with its interpretive status.

UserA scholar or student of semiotics, narrative theory, or digital humanities studying how a text guides its reader's interpretation.
InputA selected passage from the novel and the textual cues it contains.
TaskReconstruct the interpretive chain: which intrinsic knowledge, presuppositions, codes, frames and encyclopedias the cue activates, and which inferences it supports.
OutputA queryable interpretive chain ending in interpretations with explicit status: licensed, alternative, or conjectural.

The same workflow answers scholarly questions across cases: which cues require biblical knowledge? where does the text keep two predictions open at once? which interpretations does the text license, and which does it merely allow?

§ 04

Theoretical framework

Interpretive cooperation as a systemic process. Interpretation is not a magical or purely subjective act, but a cooperative pipeline governed by constraints: the reader continuously performs logical operations (abductions, deductions) to move from the linear surface of the text to its semantic actualization. The knowledge needed to understand the story is not written explicitly in the text: it resides in the reader's cooperation.

ECO

Model Author vs. Model Reader

These are not the physical persons (empirical author/reader), but textual strategies. The Model Author is the set of logical instructions woven into the text. The Model Reader is the set of interpretive conditions that the text requires to be satisfied: not an agent, but a structural position.

ECO

Encyclopedia

The background knowledge base needed to decode the text: a network of concepts and properties, comparable to a knowledge graph or a frame system such as Framester, that the reader draws on.

ECO

Inferential Walks

Interpretive bets: possible micro-worlds that the reader hypothesizes and that the text, as it unfolds, will confirm or refute. Dynamic, epistemic knowledge in motion.

ECO

The Epistemological Cut

A real reader applies a portion of their own Encyclopedia as a cognitive Lens: this act performs an epistemological cut on the linear text, isolating certain meanings and discarding others. If the reader applies the Lens intended by the Lettore Modello, the interpretation is “felicitous”; a distorted or incomplete Lens produces an arbitrary use or an interpretive deviation.

Illustrative example (toy text “The Encounter in the Woods”): if the Lettore Modello writes “hairy shadow” presupposing the Big Bad Wolf Encyclopedia, and the reader applies the Domestic Animal Lens, the epistemological cut produces a divergent interpretation. The actual corpus of the project remains The Handmaid's Tale, where this same mechanism is applied to the case of the color red. — The Epistemological Cut and the limits of interpretation

From theory to classes

Existing ontology design patterns, such as Descriptions & Situations and Cognitive Perspectivisation, were studied as a conceptual reference during the design phase. The final Econtology, however, was built from scratch: each of Eco's concepts is modeled as a dedicated OWL class in the lig: namespace, connected by properties that mirror the interpretive process.

Methodological note. Under OWL 2 open-world semantics, a weak or arbitrary interpretation does not automatically generate a logical inconsistency: failing to meet a requirement often produces only a missing inference, not a contradiction. The ontology therefore encodes explicit disjointness axioms where category confusion must be detectable (e.g. ModelReader disjointWith EmpiricalReader), while the validation of the competency questions relies on the eleven SPARQL queries, including three negative competency questions.

§ 05

Methodology

The project follows the eXtreme Design (XD) methodology: proceeding incrementally from stories (annotated concrete excerpts) to Competency Questions, to modeling in Protégé, to unit testing (SPARQL queries).

Stories collected

STORY 1

The Ceremony

Euphemisms and the conflict between ordinary encyclopedic sub-codes and those required by Gilead.

STORY 2

The name “Offred”

Presupposition of a piece of information (the real name) never made explicit in the text.

STORY 3

“Trust me” / the Eyes

An ambiguous speech act, intentional cooperative indeterminacy, coexisting alternative interpretations.

STORY 4

The color-coded clothing system

An enumerated role→color system, with two nested levels of Model Reader: the one internal to Offred's voice vs. the one belonging to the book as a whole, which includes the closing Historical Notes and reveals the extra-textual historical reference to internment camps.

Four annotated interpretive cases selected from the novel.

Enrichment

Unit testing

Competency Questions

  1. Which textual fragments and cues ground the interpretive inferences modeled in the Knowledge Graph?
  2. Which textual cues require intrinsic knowledge?
  3. Which presuppositions are triggered by textual cues?
  4. Which interpretive resources, such as codes, frames, and encyclopedias, are activated by textual cues?
  5. Which interpretations are licensed by the inferences supported by textual cues?
  6. What is the interpretive status of each interpretation?
  7. Which predictions and probability disjunctions are generated by interpretive inferences in ambiguous cases?
  8. Which Model Reader strategies or levels are presupposed by the narrative text?

Negative Competency Questions

Negative CQs verify what the Knowledge Graph must not assert: they protect the model from overclaiming.

  1. Are empirical readers or empirical authors treated as Model Reader or Model Author? (expected: no)
  2. Is the P.O.W. origin of the Handmaids' red clothing modeled as direct narrative fact? (expected: no, conjectural)
  3. Are alternative predictions in ambiguous cases collapsed into one certain outcome? (expected: no)
§ 06

The interpretive cases

Each case is an interpretive operation, not a plot summary. Its textual cues ask the Model Reader to activate intrinsic knowledge, trigger presuppositions, and draw inferences that license (or deliberately suspend) an interpretation. Click a case to explore it.

The interpretive problem

Gilead frames the Ceremony as a religious reproductive ritual, built on the biblical reading of Rachel, Leah and Bilhah (“Give me children, or else I die”) and on silent prayer. Offred's narration exposes the coercion hidden behind this frame, and rejects every ordinary category: not “making love”, not “copulating” (“only one is involved”), and even “rape” is presented as insufficient.

What the Model Reader must do

  • Activate biblical knowledge to understand why Rachel, Leah and Bilhah serve as justification.
  • Recognize how reading, prayer and blessing sacralize the act as a formalized institutional ceremony.
  • Accept that ordinary categories (love, reciprocal sex, individual violence) fail to classify the event.

Licensed interpretations

licensed The Ceremony as an institutionalized reproductive ritual, religiously framed and ethically coercive.
licensed Gilead uses religion to legitimize reproductive coercion.

The interpretive problem

“Offred” is not simply a character name: it is an interpretive cue. The Handmaids secretly exchange personal names at the Red Centre (“Alma. Janine. Dolores. Moira. June.”); Offred declares she has “another name”, forbidden and kept hidden like buried treasure; and in the Historical Notes, Professor Pieixoto explains that “Offred”, like “Ofglen” and “Ofwarren”, is a patronymic: possessive preposition plus the Commander's first name. The narrator's original name remains unavailable, and its absence is part of the meaning.

What the Model Reader must do

  • Infer that an original personal name exists, and that the text deliberately withholds it.
  • Read personal names as surviving traces of identity within Gilead's system of erasure.
  • Retrospectively reframe “Offred” as an institutional name encoding possession and belonging.

Interpretations

licensed Gilead erases personal identity by replacing women's names with patronymic names of possession.
licensed Offred's original name exists in the fictional world but remains unspecified.
alternative “June” may be Offred's original name, a reading the text never confirms and that the KG does not model as licensed.

The interpretive problem

Nick's position remains uncertain for both Offred and the reader. Early on, his wink may be friendliness, a test, or surveillance (“Perhaps he is an Eye”). At the end the black van arrives. Nick claims the men are Mayday, calls Offred by her real name, and says “Trust me”, which “has never been a talisman, carries no guarantee”. The claim of “violation of state secrets” sounds official but proves nothing. Offred steps up “into the darkness within; or else the light”.

What the Model Reader must do

  • Connect early cues to late ones and hold competing predictions open: rescue via Mayday, or arrest by the Eyes.
  • Activate knowledge about surveillance, clandestine resistance, betrayal, and trust under coercion.
  • Recognize that the ending keeps the ambiguity open as a probability disjunction instead of resolving it.

Interpretations

licensed The final departure is a licensed ambiguity, suspended between rescue and destruction.
licensed Trust is constructed as an uncertain interpretive act under authoritarian surveillance.
alternative Nick may be a rescuer connected to Mayday.
alternative Nick may be an Eye; the van may signal arrest.

The interpretive problem

Gilead never publishes its dress code, yet the text builds it into its Model Reader's competence through dispersed cues: a dressing scene, an umbrella stand, a street enumeration, a room where people are named only by their colors (“One kneeling woman in red, one seated woman in blue, two in green…”). By the Ceremony tableau the code has become presupposed competence: the text uses the code instead of stating it.

RED · Handmaids BLUE · Wives GREEN · Marthas STRIPES · Econowives BLACK · Commanders / Widows

Two levels of Model Reader

The Historical Notes (a symposium held in 2195) retroactively reframe the whole narration as a recovered, edited artifact. This creates a second level of Model Reader: the reader of Offred's voice sees the colors as Gilead's internal code, while the reader of the whole book must also activate extra-textual historical knowledge. Pieixoto attributes the Handmaids' red to the uniforms of German P.O.W.s in Canadian WWII camps, but the attribution is hedged (“seems to have”), so it is modeled as scholarly conjecture inside the fiction, never as narrative fact.

Interpretations

licensed Gilead's chromatic code renders social roles, gender hierarchy and household position visually legible, substituting function for personal identity.
licensed At the second level, the code is a designed, historically derivative artifact (“its genius was synthesis”).
alternative Red as symbolic marking of the Handmaids' reproductive function.
conjectural The P.O.W. origin of the Handmaids' red.

§ 07

The ontology: Econtology

The Econtology is the reusable TBox of the project: an OWL 2 ontology, serialized in Turtle, that formalizes the key concepts of Lector in fabula: Model Author and Model Reader, textual cues and gaps, encyclopedia, codes, frames, intrinsic knowledge, inferential walks, possible worlds, and the distinction between interpretation and use. It is independent of any specific text and is instantiated by the Handmaid's Tale knowledge graph.

SWRL rules

The ontology includes three SWRL rules that materialize interpretive chains in the Knowledge Graph. These rules infer which textual fragments ground interpretive inferences, which textual cues contribute to interpretations, and which intrinsic knowledge supports specific inferences. The rules were tested on the Knowledge Graph through Drools in Protégé, producing 28 groundsInference assertions, 29 contributesToInterpretation assertions, and 48 knowledgeSupportsInference assertions.

The Knowledge Graph imports the Econtology and populates it with the four interpretive cases, in the dedicated namespace hmt: (https://lectoringraphula.org/handmaid#). It models how specific textual cues activate intrinsic knowledge, presuppositions, inferences, and interpretations according to Eco's theory. It is a case study, not a plot database.

§ 09

SPARQL queries

Eleven SPARQL queries validate the Competency Questions (01–08) and the Negative Competency Questions (09–11) against the Knowledge Graph. Click on a question to open it and see the SPARQL code. All queries are available for download.

CQ1Grounding inferences01_grounding_inferences.rq

Pattern: TextFragment → containsCue → TextualCue → supportsInference → Inference. Lists the fragments, cues and inferences across the four cases.

SELECT ?fragmentLabel ?source ?cueLabel ?cueText ?inferenceLabel
WHERE {
  ?fragment lig:containsCue ?cue .
  ?cue lig:supportsInference ?inference .
  # + OPTIONAL labels, source references, textual content
}
CQ2Intrinsic knowledge02_intrinsic_knowledge.rq

Which implicit knowledge is required by each textual cue?

SELECT ?fragmentLabel ?cueLabel ?cueText ?knowledgeLabel
WHERE {
  ?fragment lig:containsCue ?cue .
  ?cue lig:requiresIntrinsicKnowledge ?knowledge .
}
CQ3Presuppositions03_presuppositions.rq

Which implicit presuppositions are triggered by each textual cue?

SELECT ?fragmentLabel ?cueLabel ?cueText ?presuppositionLabel
WHERE {
  ?fragment lig:containsCue ?cue .
  ?cue lig:triggersPresupposition ?presupposition .
}
CQ4Interpretive resources04_interpretive_resources.rq

Codes, frames and encyclopedias activated by cues, via a three-branch UNION.

WHERE {
  ?fragment lig:containsCue ?cue .
  { ?cue lig:activatesCode ?resource . }
  UNION { ?cue lig:activatesFrame ?resource . }
  UNION { ?cue lig:activatesEncyclopedia ?resource . }
}
CQ5Licensed interpretations05_licensed_interpretations.rq

The full interpretive chain: cue → inference → interpretation, with sequence numbers and status.

WHERE {
  ?cue lig:supportsInference ?inference .
  ?inference lig:licensesInterpretation ?interpretation .
}
CQ6Interpretive status06_interpretive_status.rq

Verifies that the KG distinguishes licensed, alternative and conjectural interpretations.

SELECT DISTINCT ?interpretationLabel ?status
WHERE { ?interpretation lig:hasInterpretiveStatus ?status . }
CQ7Predictions & disjunctions07_predictions_disjunctions.rq

How the KG represents ambiguity: inference → prediction → probability disjunction → alternative predictions.

WHERE {
  ?inference lig:generatesPrediction ?prediction .
  ?prediction lig:opensProbabilityDisjunction ?disjunction .
  ?disjunction lig:hasAlternativePrediction ?alternativePrediction .
}
CQ8Model Reader levels08_model_reader_levels.rq

Which Model Reader strategies or levels are presupposed by the narrative text (first-level narration vs. the book as a whole, including the Historical Notes).

WHERE { ?text lig:presupposesModelReader ?modelReader . }
NCQ1No empirical confusion09_no_empirical_confusion.rq

Model Reader/Author must never coincide with empirical readers/authors. Expected result: 0 rows.

WHERE {
  { ?entity rdf:type lig:ModelReader , lig:EmpiricalReader . }
  UNION
  { ?entity rdf:type lig:ModelAuthor , lig:EmpiricalAuthor . }
}
NCQ2P.O.W. origin is not fact10_pow_not_direct_fact.rq

The P.O.W. origin of the Handmaids' red must be modeled as conjecture. Expected result: one row, status “conjectural”.

WHERE {
  hmt:POWUniformOriginConjecture lig:hasInterpretiveStatus ?status .
}
NCQ3No collapsed predictions11_no_collapsed_predictions.rq

The probability disjunctions of the ambiguous ending must preserve their alternative predictions instead of collapsing into one certain outcome.

WHERE {
  ?disjunction lig:hasAlternativePrediction ?alternativePrediction .
  FILTER ( ?disjunction = hmt:NickRoleProbabilityDisjunction ||
           ?disjunction = hmt:FinalOutcomeProbabilityDisjunction )
}
§ 10

POLANYI++

POLANYI++ was used as an external comparison tool for inspecting implicit knowledge in selected textual cues. The goal was not to automatically validate the ontology, but to compare the implicit assumptions suggested by the tool with the IntrinsicKnowledge, Presupposition and Inference individuals manually modeled in the Knowledge Graph. Two cues were chosen, one from a withholding case and one from an ambiguity case.

Cue 1: “My name isn't Offred”

MODE: EXTRACT · METHOD: m1 TACIT · TASK: EXPLAIN · HEURISTICS: b4 PRESUPPOSITION, b5 IMPLICATURE, b12 PERSPECTIVE

POLANYI++ reads the passage as a first-person reflection on a forbidden name. It highlights the contrast between the imposed name “Offred” and a hidden personal one, a conflict between institutional control and personal identity. It also finds the presuppositions that a true name exists, that its use is forbidden, and that it may one day be recovered. The image of the name as “treasure” and “amulet” implies its value and protective power. These results converge with the KG entities HiddenNamePresupposition, HiddenRealNameKnowledge and OffredIdentityInference.

Cue 2: “Perhaps he is an Eye”

HEURISTICS INCLUDING: b10 THEORY OF MIND

The tool notes that the narrator constantly attributes mental states to Nick, that the interaction is open to multiple readings (friendly, testing, or surveillance), and that the question implies danger and disorientation. This maps directly onto the KG entities SurveillanceKnowledge, FriendlyBehaviorMayBeTestPresupposition, NickEyeSuspicionInference and NickRoleProbabilityDisjunction.

§ 11

Conclusion

Lector in Graphula shows that Eco's theory of interpretive cooperation can be made computable without being flattened. The ontology represents the reader's work as queryable entities, and the knowledge graph shows on four cases from The Handmaid's Tale that a text's blank spaces can be modeled with the same rigor as its explicit statements.

The model is also honest about what it knows: interpretations carry explicit statuses, ambiguous endings remain probability disjunctions, conjectures stay conjectures, and negative competency questions guard against overclaiming. The graph does not decide what the text leaves open: it represents the openness itself.

“And so I step up, into the darkness within; or else the light.” — Margaret Atwood, The Handmaid's Tale
