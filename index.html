Lector in Graphula

Modeling Eco’s “Lettore Modello” and Intrinsic Knowledge in The Handmaid’s Tale

A Semantic Web project that formalizes selected aspects of Umberto Eco’s theory of interpretive cooperation and instantiates them as a Knowledge Graph for four interpretive cases from Margaret Atwood’s novel.

01

Overview

The project

Abstract

Lector in Graphula, an ontology of Umberto Eco’s theory of the “Lettore Modello”, was developed within the course Knowledge Representation and Extraction, taught by Professor Aldo Gangemi during the academic year 2025/2026 of the Master’s Degree in Digital Humanities and Digital Knowledge at the University of Bologna.

The project uses Semantic Web technologies to model selected implicit interpretive processes that occur while reading a narrative text.

Eco’s theory of interpretive cooperation provides the conceptual framework. The ontology formalizes concepts such as textual cues, intrinsic knowledge, presuppositions, inferences, predictions and interpretations.

The Knowledge Graph instantiates these concepts on selected excerpts from The Handmaid’s Tale.

Research question

How can Eco’s theory of the Model Reader be formalized through an ontology and instantiated as a Knowledge Graph in order to represent the intrinsic interpretive knowledge required to understand selected excerpts from The Handmaid’s Tale?

Why The Handmaid’s Tale?

The Handmaid’s Tale strongly depends on implicit and intrinsic knowledge. Gilead’s rules, hierarchies, rituals and ideological mechanisms are not explained all at once. They are gradually reconstructed through textual cues, omissions, repetitions and ambiguities.

The novel presupposes a reader capable of activating biblical references, dystopian conventions, knowledge about gender and power relations, institutional language, social classification systems and historical allusions.

The project does not model the complete plot. It represents how selected passages guide a reader from the textual surface towards an interpretation through structured inferential processes.

Scope. The project does not model the entire novel, establish one definitive interpretation or claim to automate literary interpretation. It makes selected interpretive chains explicit, inspectable and queryable.

02

Theoretical framework

The Model Reader and the “lazy machine”

The text as a lazy machine

In Eco’s theory, a text does not state everything required for its own interpretation. It contains gaps and unsaid elements that presuppose the cooperation of a competent reader.

This textual “laziness” is not a defect. It is the condition that makes interpretation possible. The reader activates knowledge, codes, frames and inferential operations to actualize what the text leaves implicit.

Interpretive cooperation

Interpretation is understood as a constrained process rather than as unrestricted subjectivity. The reader moves from the linear manifestation of the text to its semantic actualization by activating interpretive resources, producing inferences and projecting possible developments.

lig:ModelAuthor

Model Author

The textual strategy that organizes instructions, cues, gaps and interpretive constraints.

lig:ModelReader

Model Reader

The textual strategy and set of competencies presupposed and constructed by the text.

lig:Encyclopedia

Encyclopedia

The network of background knowledge from which relevant interpretive resources are activated.

lig:TextualCue

Textual cue

A locally identifiable textual element that activates an interpretive process.

lig:IntrinsicKnowledge

Intrinsic knowledge

Knowledge required for interpretation but not fully expressed in the linear text.

lig:InferentialWalk

Inferential walk

An interpretive hypothesis or projected possible world that may later be confirmed, rejected or left open.

Critical distinction

Model Reader ≠ Empirical Reader

Model Author ≠ Empirical Author

Model Reader and Model Author are textual strategies. They are not Umberto Eco, Margaret Atwood or individual real readers.

Methodological note

Under OWL’s open-world semantics, a weak or arbitrary interpretation does not automatically produce a logical inconsistency. Missing evidence is not equivalent to a contradiction.

OWL inconsistency is therefore used only in controlled cases involving explicit constraints. Broader evaluation is carried out through SPARQL queries and negative competency questions.

Selected modeling constraints

  • ModelReader is disjoint with EmpiricalReader.
  • ModelAuthor is disjoint with EmpiricalAuthor.
  • Interpretation is disjoint with Use.
  • TextualCue is disjoint with Interpretation.
  • Code is disjoint with Frame.
  • EssentialProperty is disjoint with AccidentalProperty.

LicensedInterpretation and AlternativeInterpretation are not disjoint. An interpretation may be alternative while still being textually licensed.

Case 01 Offred’s name Identity, prohibition and institutional naming Open
“My name isn’t Offred”
Textual cue OffredNameCue
Intrinsic knowledge HiddenRealNameKnowledge
Presupposition HiddenNamePresupposition
Inference OffredIdentityInference
Interpretation GileadErasesFemaleIdentity

The reader infers that “Offred” is an imposed institutional name and that another, hidden personal name exists.

The narrator’s original name remains unspecified. Reader-supported proposals are not modeled as confirmed narrative facts.

Nick may be connected to the regime, to the resistance or to neither in a fully knowable way. The Knowledge Graph preserves this ambiguity instead of reducing it to one certain interpretation.

Case 04 Color-coded clothing Visual classification and identity control Open
“Everything except the wings around my face is red”
Textual cue InitialColorAssignmentCue
Code GileadChromaticRoleStatusCode

Clothing colors make social roles publicly readable. Individual identity is replaced by institutional function through a visible semiotic classification system.

The possible relationship between red clothing and prisoner-of-war uniforms remains conjectural rather than a direct narrative fact.

SWRL materialization

Interpretive-chain rules

Three SWRL rules materialize relations across the interpretive chains. They were tested through Drools in Protégé.

Grounding inferences 

containsCue(?fragment, ?cue)
^ supportsInference(?cue, ?inference)
→ groundsInference(?fragment, ?inference)

Produced 28 groundsInference assertions.

Contributing to interpretations 

supportsInference(?cue, ?inference)
^ licensesInterpretation(?inference, ?interpretation)
→ contributesToInterpretation(?cue, ?interpretation)

Produced 29 contributesToInterpretation assertions.

Supporting inferences with knowledge 

requiresIntrinsicKnowledge(?cue, ?knowledge)
^ supportsInference(?cue, ?inference)
→ knowledgeSupportsInference(?knowledge, ?inference)

Produced 48 knowledgeSupportsInference assertions.

06

External comparison

POLANYI++

POLANYI++ was used as an external comparison tool for inspecting implicit knowledge in selected textual cues.

Its outputs were reviewed and compared with the IntrinsicKnowledge, Presupposition, Inference, Prediction and Interpretation individuals manually modeled in the Knowledge Graph.

POLANYI++ is not part of the official Knowledge Graph and is not used as an automatic validation mechanism.

01 Offred’s name “My name isn’t Offred”

EXTRACT · m1 TACIT · EXPLAIN · b4 PRESUPPOSITION · b5 IMPLICATURE · b12 PERSPECTIVE

POLANYI++ identifies a contrast between an imposed institutional name and a hidden personal name. It highlights the existence of a true name, the prohibition of its use and its possible future recovery.

The output overlaps with the Knowledge Graph’s representation of memory, identity suppression and residual personal agency.

  • OffredNameCue
  • HiddenRealNameKnowledge
  • HiddenNamePresupposition
  • OffredIdentityInference
  • GileadErasesFemaleIdentity
02 The Ceremony “Nor does rape cover it”

The EXTRACT step produced a Turtle graph without a narrative synthesis. A DISCOVER step was therefore applied to the generated graph.

The synthesis characterized the Ceremony through objectification and lack of agency. These observations overlap with the Knowledge Graph’s representation of constrained choice and institutionalized reproductive coercion.

03 Nick and the Eyes “Perhaps he is an Eye”

EXTRACT · m1 TACIT · EXPLAIN · b4 PRESUPPOSITION · b5 IMPLICATURE · b10 THEORY OF MIND · b12 PERSPECTIVE · b13 HYPOTHETICAL · b26 LACUNA

POLANYI++ identifies an ambiguous interaction filtered through a suspicious and self-protective perspective. Nick’s intentions may be friendly, manipulative, testing or connected to surveillance.

The Knowledge Graph formalizes this uncertainty through predictions, probability disjunctions and alternative interpretations.

  • SurveillanceKnowledge
  • TrustUnderSurveillanceKnowledge
  • FriendlyBehaviorMayBeTestPresupposition
  • NickEyeSuspicionInference
  • NickRoleProbabilityDisjunction
04 Color-coded clothing “Everything except the wings around my face is red”

POLANYI++ generated a Turtle graph but no human-readable synthesis. The graph was therefore interpreted manually for the methodological comparison.

The output suggests implicit assumptions about color as a social marker, institutional dress, visible classification and identity control.

This overlaps with the Knowledge Graph’s representation of clothing as a semiotic code that makes institutional roles publicly readable.
