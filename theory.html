Lector in Graphula / Theoretical framework

Umberto Eco · Interpretive cooperation · Ontology design

From the “lazy machine” to the Knowledge Graph

This page presents the theoretical concepts that informed the project and distinguishes the elements directly formalized in the ontology from the broader semiotic background.

Start reading Return to the ontology

Scope

A selective formalization

Lector in Graphula translates selected aspects of Umberto Eco’s theory of interpretive cooperation into a formal and computational model using Semantic Web technologies. It was developed for the course Knowledge Representation and Extraction, taught by Professor Aldo Gangemi during the 2025/2026 academic year of the Master’s Degree in Digital Humanities and Digital Knowledge at the University of Bologna.

The project does not claim to reproduce the whole of Lector in fabula. It focuses on concepts that can clarify how textual cues, presupposed competencies, intrinsic knowledge, inferences, predictions and interpretations interact in selected narrative passages.

These relationships are represented through an OWL ontology and instantiated in a Knowledge Graph based on selected extracts from Margaret Atwood’s The Handmaid’s Tale.

01

Interpretive cooperation

Interpretation as a constrained activity

In this framework, interpretation is neither the passive recovery of a meaning already written in full nor an unrestricted projection by an individual reader. The text provides a sequence of expressions, cues, gaps and constraints that call for the cooperation of a reader equipped with particular competencies.

The reader moves from the linear manifestation of the text towards its semantic actualization. This transition requires the activation of codes, frames, encyclopedic knowledge, presuppositions and inferential operations.

The ontology represents this cooperation as a chain of explicit relations. A textual fragment may contain a cue; the cue may require intrinsic knowledge, trigger a presupposition and support an inference; the inference may then license an interpretation or generate a prediction.

Not unrestricted

Textual constraints matter

Interpretations differ in status because they are not equally supported by the text. The model therefore distinguishes licensed, alternative and conjectural outcomes.

Not automatic

The graph represents, it does not replace reading

The Knowledge Graph makes selected interpretive paths inspectable and queryable. It does not calculate the one correct meaning of a literary text.

02

Textual strategies

The text as a “lazy machine”

A narrative text cannot state every premise, consequence and background relation needed for its interpretation. It is “lazy” because it leaves part of its semantic work to the reader. Its incompleteness is productive: gaps and unsaid elements activate cooperation.

This cooperation is organized through two complementary textual strategies.

lig:ModelAuthor

Model Author

The organizing strategy of the text: the way cues, instructions, omissions and interpretive constraints are arranged.

lig:ModelReader

Model Reader

The set of competencies and interpretive operations that the text presupposes and progressively constructs in order to be actualized.

Accessible example

A game designer and an expected player

A game designer constructs levels, rules and clues. The expected player is not one empirical person, but the competence required to recognize those rules and use the available controls. In a comparable way, a narrative organizes textual cues and presupposes a reader capable of activating them.

Critical distinction

Model Reader ≠ Empirical Reader

Model Author ≠ Empirical Author

Model Reader and Model Author are textual strategies. The empirical reader is an actual person reading the text; the empirical author is the historical person who produced it.

03

Interpretive resources

The Encyclopedia and the Q Model

Understanding a text requires more than dictionary definitions. The reader activates an Encyclopedia: a culturally structured network of knowledge, codes, frames, associations and possible contexts.

This Encyclopedia should not be reduced to a private list of facts stored in an individual mind. In the project, it is treated as the network of competencies that the text presupposes its Model Reader can activate.

Eco’s discussion of a Q Model helps explain why semantic competence is better understood as a network than as a rigid dictionary. The Knowledge Graph adopts a graph-based representation, but it does not claim to implement the entire theoretical Q Model.

Accessible example

From “apple” to a network of contexts

A dictionary may identify an apple as a fruit. Encyclopedic competence can also connect it to Newton, the biblical story of Adam and Eve, William Tell, food practices or a technology company. A text activates only the relations relevant to its current context.

lig:Encyclopedia

The general network of interpretable knowledge.

lig:Code

A system of correlations used to interpret signs.

lig:Frame

A structured scenario that organizes expectations.

lig:IntrinsicKnowledge

Knowledge required by a cue but not fully stated in the text.

04

Selective actualization

Magnification and narcotization

Encyclopedic competence contains more information than any particular passage needs. Interpretation therefore requires a selective operation. Some semantic properties are foregrounded because they are relevant to the current topic, while others remain temporarily unactualized.

Magnification foregrounds properties that are useful for the current interpretation. Narcotization keeps other available properties in the background. A narcotized property is not false or erased: it may become relevant later when the textual context changes.

Accessible example

A raised fist in a confrontation

When a character raises a fist during an argument, the reader may foreground aggression, threat and bodily movement. The biological fact that the character is breathing remains compatible with the scene, but it is not needed to interpret that moment.

Modeling status

Magnification and narcotization belong to the theoretical background of the project, but the current TBox does not contain dedicated classes named Magnification or Narcotization.

05

Pragmatic meaning

The operational precept and the habit

Eco’s broader semiotic framework draws on the pragmatic tradition associated with C. S. Peirce. Meaning is not exhausted by a static definition: it can also be understood through the operations, expectations and practical consequences associated with a sign.

An operational precept explains how a concept guides action or interpretation. A habit is a stabilized disposition to interpret or respond in a certain way. These ideas help explain why competence is procedural as well as declarative.

Accessible example

A practical description of a material

Knowing only a material’s numerical properties may not be enough to recognize or use it. An operational description also explains what to do, which tests to perform and what consequences to expect. Meaning therefore includes rules for possible action.

Modeling status

The current ontology does not introduce dedicated OperationalPrecept or Habit classes. These notions provide theoretical context for the project’s treatment of interpretive competence and inference.

06

Interpretive operations

Inferential walks, predictions and open alternatives

When the linear text does not immediately provide a complete solution, the reader may perform an inferential walk. This operation temporarily leaves the explicit sequence of the text in order to activate encyclopedic knowledge and construct a hypothesis.

A hypothesis about what may happen next is represented as a prediction. Several predictions may remain simultaneously available, forming a probability disjunction rather than one certain outcome.

Continued reading may confirm one hypothesis, reject it or leave the alternatives unresolved. The Nick and the Eyes case uses this structure to preserve the opposition between arrest and rescue.

Accessible example

Gunshots at the end of a chapter

A reader may activate knowledge about danger and construct several possible continuations: the protagonist may have been injured, another character may have fired, or the sound may have a different cause. The next passage changes the status of these predictions.

07

Narrative constructions

Possible worlds and S-necessary properties

Predictions and interpretations may construct possible worlds: organized states of affairs in which individuals possess properties and stand in relations. Narrative worlds need not reproduce every rule of the empirical world, but they must remain intelligible according to their own constraints.

Essential properties support the identification of an individual across possible worlds. Accidental properties may vary without changing that identification. S-necessary properties are structurally necessary within a specific world or world state.

Accessible example

A local rule in a fantasy world

A story may establish that its vampires are harmed by sunlight. This is not a rule about the empirical world. Once introduced as a structural constraint of that narrative world, however, later events must be interpreted in relation to it.

lig:EssentialProperty

Essential

Supports cross-world identification of an individual.

lig:AccidentalProperty

Accidental

May vary while the individual remains identifiable.

lig:SNecessaryProperty

S-necessary

Structurally required within a particular world state.

Interpretive scope

The mapping is deliberately selective. The ontology captures the concepts required by the project’s Competency Questions and case studies rather than attempting an exhaustive formalization of Eco’s semiotic theory.
